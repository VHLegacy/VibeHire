Why Every Malaysian Needs a Will — And Why Most Don't Have One

📅 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ VibeHire Editorial

There's a conversation most Malaysians avoid. Not because it's difficult — but because it feels uncomfortable. The conversation about what happens after you're gone.

Who gets your savings? Who looks after your children? What happens to your share in the family business? Most people assume their family will figure it out. Most of the time, they're wrong — and by then, it's too late to fix.

The Numbers Tell a Sobering Story

Malaysia has one of the highest rates of unclaimed assets in the region. According to Amanah Raya Berhad, billions of ringgit in assets remain unclaimed every year — money, property, investments, and savings that belong to families but can't be accessed because there's no clear legal instruction on who they belong to.

The reason? Most Malaysians don't have a will.

It's not because they don't care about their families. It's because writing a will feels like something you do when you're old. Something you'll get to later. Something that doesn't feel urgent — until it suddenly is.

"The best time to write a will was the day you had something worth protecting. The second best time is today."

What Actually Happens If You Die Without a Will in Malaysia

When a person dies without a will in Malaysia — a situation known legally as dying "intestate" — their assets don't automatically go to their spouse or children. Instead, the Distribution Act 1958 kicks in and decides for them.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

  • If you're married with children, your spouse gets one third. Your children split the rest — not your spouse.
  • If you have no children, your parents may be entitled to a share — even if you and your spouse have built everything together.
  • If you own a business, your shares may be frozen while the courts sort out who owns what — potentially disrupting operations for months or years.
  • Your family will need to apply to court to be appointed as administrators of your estate — a process that can take years and cost significant legal fees.

None of this is what most people intend. But without a will, intention means nothing. The law decides.

⚠️ For Muslim Malaysians

Faraid — Islamic inheritance law — governs the distribution of a Muslim's estate in Malaysia. While a will (wasiat) can be used for up to one-third of assets and for non-Muslim beneficiaries, specific rules apply. Speaking with a qualified Islamic estate planner is essential to ensure your wishes are carried out correctly under both civil and Syariah law.

Three Groups Who Need a Will More Than They Realise

1. Business Owners

If you own shares in a company — even a small Sdn. Bhd. — those shares are part of your estate. Without a will naming who inherits them, your business could be tied up in court while your family waits and your operations suffer. A will lets you decide whether the business continues, who runs it, and whether it should be sold.

2. Parents of Young Children

A will is not just about money. It's also where you name a guardian for your children if something happens to you and your spouse. Without this, the court decides who raises your children. That's a decision no parent should leave to chance.

3. Anyone With Property or Savings

You don't need to be wealthy to need a will. If you own a house, a car, an EPF account, or a savings account — you have assets worth protecting. Even a simple will ensures those assets go exactly where you want them to, without delays or disputes.

Why People Keep Putting It Off

The most common reasons Malaysians don't have a will come down to three things:

  • It feels morbid. Thinking about death is uncomfortable. But writing a will is actually an act of love — it's protecting the people you care about from unnecessary pain and confusion.
  • They think it's complicated. It doesn't have to be. Modern will writing services like DWS2U make the process simple, guided, and accessible — without needing to hire a lawyer for a basic will.
  • They think it's expensive. Writing a basic will in Malaysia costs far less than most people expect. The cost of not having one — in legal fees, court applications, and family disputes — is almost always much higher.
